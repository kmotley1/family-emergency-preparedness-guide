Go-Bag Checklist: What Actually Goes In It

A printable, prioritized checklist sorted by category. Tier 1 items you need before anything else. Tier 2 additions once the foundation is covered. What to skip entirely.

A go-bag is not a camping pack. It is not a survival fantasy kit. It is simply a small backpack that lets you leave your home quickly and function independently for about 72 hours.

Most families either under-prepare (a flashlight and a granola bar) or over-prepare (40 pounds of gear they will never carry). The goal is something in the middle: a bag that is practical, portable, and ready at a moment’s notice.

A good rule: if you had to leave your home in five minutes, could you grab one bag and survive three days without returning?

Tier 1 — The Non-Negotiables

These items cover the four survival priorities: water, warmth, medical care, and information. Build this layer first.

  • Water (1–2 liters per person)
  • Water filter or purification tablets
  • Non-perishable food (3 days)
  • Energy bars, canned meals, or ready-to-eat rations
  • First aid kit
  • Bandages, antiseptic wipes, pain medication
  • Flashlight or headlamp
  • Extra batteries
  • Emergency blanket or compact sleeping bag
  • Phone charger + battery pack
  • Copies of important documents
  • ID, insurance, emergency contacts
  • Cash in small bills ($100–$300)

With just these items, you can leave your home and operate for several days without relying on outside systems.

Tier 2 — Practical Upgrades

Once the essentials are covered, these additions improve comfort and resilience.

  • Multi-tool or small knife
  • Hand-crank or battery radio
  • Portable phone charger
  • Work gloves
  • Dust masks
  • Change of clothes
  • Basic hygiene kit (toothbrush, wipes, sanitizer)
  • Notebook and pen

None of these items are strictly necessary — but they make stressful situations significantly easier to manage.

Tier 3 — Situation-Specific Items

These depend on your household.

  • Prescription medications
  • Infant supplies
  • Pet food and leash
  • Glasses or contact lenses
  • Seasonal clothing

What You Can Skip

The internet is full of extreme gear lists. Most of them assume wilderness survival scenarios that are extremely unlikely.

In real emergencies, the biggest problems are usually infrastructure failure — power outages, evacuation orders, supply disruptions — not wilderness survival.

You probably do not need:

  • Large survival knives
  • Military-grade gear
  • Cooking stoves
  • Huge food stockpiles
  • Heavy tools

If the bag becomes too heavy, you will leave it behind — which defeats the purpose entirely.

Where Your Go-Bag Should Live

A go-bag only works if it is immediately accessible.

  • Near your home exit
  • In a hall closet
  • Under the bed
  • In your vehicle (optional second bag)

Avoid storing it in attics, basements, or deep storage areas.

Every adult in the household should have their own go-bag. Children should have smaller versions with age-appropriate supplies.

Maintenance: The Step Everyone Forgets

Preparedness systems fail when they are never updated.

  • Check food and water every 6 months
  • Replace expired medications
  • Update documents
  • Swap seasonal clothing

A 10-minute review twice a year keeps your bag ready indefinitely.

The Goal Is Simplicity

The purpose of preparedness is not fear. It is reducing decision-making when stress is high.

A simple go-bag turns chaos into a checklist.

And once you have one, you will probably never need it.

But if you ever do, you will be very glad it exists.
