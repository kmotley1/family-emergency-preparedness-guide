Emergency Preparedness for Apartment Dwellers

No yard. No basement. No room for 200 gallons of water. Most preparedness guides are written for homeowners with garages, which means renters get advice that doesn't fit. This covers what actually applies — and skips everything that doesn't.

March 2026 · 5 min read · From the Emergency Preparedness Guide

About 44 million households in the United States rent their homes. The majority of those households live in apartment buildings — no storage unit, no garage, shared hallways, building rules about what can be kept on balconies, and landlords who control what modifications can be made to the space. A preparedness framework built around a four-bedroom house with a basement does not translate.

The core preparedness goals are the same regardless of housing type: 72 hours of self-sufficiency, a plan for where to go and how to communicate, and access to cash and critical documents when systems are down. The difference for apartment dwellers is how those goals get met within real space and access constraints.

Working With Limited Space

Storage in an apartment is a constraint, not a dealbreaker. The goal is not to store everything — it is to store the right things densely. Most of what a household needs for a 72-hour emergency fits in a single large backpack and a small closet shelf.

Water is the heaviest and bulkiest item in any emergency supply, and also the most non-negotiable. The standard minimum is one gallon per person per day. For a single person, three gallons covers 72 hours — that is six standard 16-oz water bottles plus change, or three 1-liter bottles. For a couple, six gallons. This fits on a single closet shelf or under a bathroom sink. You do not need a 55-gallon drum. You need the right quantity stored in the right containers, rotated every six months.

The apartment-specific water answer

WaterBOB bathtub bladders ($30) can hold up to 100 gallons using a standard bathtub in the event of an extended disruption. Fill it when a situation is escalating, before municipal water is affected. One bladder per bathtub, filled in advance, covers a household's water needs for weeks. It stores flat in a closet until needed.

Food storage follows the same logic as the rotating pantry approach, just scaled to what fits. A single shelf or an underbed storage container holds 2 to 4 weeks of shelf-stable staples for one person: rice, canned beans, canned fish, oats, nut butter, crackers. Eat from it regularly and replace what you use. The supply stays current without requiring a dedicated pantry room.

Evacuation Specific to Building Living

Apartment evacuation introduces variables that single-family home evacuation does not. Elevators may be down. Stairwells may be crowded or blocked. Building exits may be locked or inaccessible from specific floors. These are not reasons to skip evacuation planning — they are reasons to make it more specific.

Walk your building's stairwells before you need them. Identify all exits, not just the one closest to your unit. Know which exit puts you closest to your vehicle if you have one, and which direction that exit faces relative to where you would be going. In a building fire or structural emergency, familiarity with the physical layout is worth more than any amount of gear.

Floor matters

If you live above the sixth floor, elevator dependency is a real constraint during power outages and fire evacuations. A go-bag that you cannot carry down twelve flights of stairs under stress is not a go-bag. Weight-test your bag on the stairs before you need to use it.

Your building's evacuation plan is posted somewhere — typically near elevators or in the lobby. Read it once. It tells you the designated assembly point for your building, which is different from your personal meeting point. You need both: where the building expects you to go, and where your household will regroup if members are separated.

The Apartment Go-Bag

The go-bag principle is the same in any housing type: 72 hours of supplies in a single bag, packed and ready, maintained twice a year. The apartment version has one additional constraint — it lives in a space where there is no mudroom or garage, so it has to fit somewhere accessible without being in the way.

A 30-liter daypack kept in a closet near the front door is the right format. Not under the bed in a container that takes four minutes to retrieve. Not in a storage unit in a different building. Near the door, accessible in under a minute.

Apartment go-bag — Tier 1 contents
  • Tier 1 Water — 1 liter minimum per person The bag version. More water lives in your unit. The bag carries what you need for the first few hours before you reach your destination.
  • Tier 1 72 hours of food per person Dense, no-cook options: nut butter packets, bars, jerky, crackers. Nothing that requires boiling water as a first step.
  • Tier 1 Cash — $200 minimum in small bills When ATMs are down and cards don't work, cash is what functions. Small bills only — nothing larger than $20.
  • Tier 1 Copies of critical documents ID, passport, insurance cards, lease agreement, one bank account page. Photocopies in a waterproof sleeve. Originals stay in your unit in a known location.
  • Tier 1 Medications — 3-day supply Any prescription that cannot be missed. Keep a small supply in the bag and rotate it so it stays current.
  • Tier 1 Phone charger and power bank Keep the power bank charged. A dead power bank in a go-bag is the same as no power bank.
  • Tier 1 Basic first aid kit Bandages, antiseptic wipes, medical tape, pain reliever, any household-specific items. Pre-assembled kits work fine — check expiration dates twice a year.
  • Tier 1 Printed contact list and emergency plan The five most important phone numbers written on paper. Your meeting points and evacuation destinations. Does not require a charged device to access.
  • Tier 2 Headlamp with extra batteries Stairwell and hallway lighting fails during power outages. A headlamp keeps hands free. Flashlights on phones drain batteries fast.
  • Tier 2 Change of clothes and sturdy shoes One set. Wool or synthetic, not cotton. Closed-toe shoes capable of walking on debris. People evacuate in what they are wearing, which is often not appropriate for extended movement.

Your Building as a Resource

One thing apartment living provides that single-family homes often lack is proximity to other people. During an extended disruption, that proximity is a resource if you have used the time before the emergency to identify who your neighbors are.

You do not need a formal neighborhood preparedness group. You need to know the names of two or three neighbors well enough to knock on their door. An elderly neighbor on your floor who would need help evacuating. A neighbor with a vehicle who might be willing to share a trip out if roads are accessible. Someone with a different set of skills or supplies than you have. These relationships take ten minutes to start and do not require any shared ideology or formal structure.

The building's management office is also worth knowing before an emergency. Find out who to contact about building-wide emergencies, whether the building has a generator, and what the building's policy is on extended power outages. Most residents never find this out until they need it.

Working Within Renter Constraints

Renters cannot bolt heavy furniture to walls in most leases, cannot install permanent security modifications, and cannot make structural changes. These constraints are real but narrower than they seem.

Furniture straps that use existing wall studs without permanent modification are allowed under most leases and prevent heavy bookcases and appliances from falling during seismic events. A door security bar — the kind that wedges under a doorknob — requires no installation and adds significant resistance to forced entry. A portable carbon monoxide detector and a smoke detector in each room are renter-appropriate and not dependent on a landlord's timeline.

The renter's version of home hardening

Focus on what leaves with you when you move: portable security tools, standalone detectors, and furniture anchoring methods that do not damage walls permanently. Everything in your go-bag travels with you regardless of where you live. That is the most durable form of preparedness for anyone renting.

Your lease agreement belongs in your go-bag document sleeve. During a displacement event, proof of tenancy matters for insurance claims, FEMA assistance eligibility, and re-entry to your building if access is restricted. Most renters do not think of this until after they need it.

Where to Start

If you have done none of this, the order that matters most for apartment dwellers is: bag first, water second, plan third. The bag takes one afternoon to put together using what you already own plus a few specific purchases. The water takes ten minutes to set up once you have the right containers. The plan takes 45 minutes of conversation with whoever shares your household.

All three are done before the week is out if you start today. None of them require a yard, a basement, or a landlord's permission.
